## The Move to Virginia Wesleyan

The Virginia MOCA's move to Virginia Wesleyan University marks a significant step in its evolution. But why the move, and what can we expect from the MOCA in its new home? The **Virginia MOCA Bids Farewell** to its old location, but is looking forward.

### Why the Move?

While the Virginia MOCA has thrived at its Parks Avenue location, several factors contributed to the decision to relocate:

*   **Building Repairs and Maintenance:** The existing facility requires significant repairs and maintenance work, estimated at around \$900,000, according to the city.
*   **Environmental Constraints:** The Parks Avenue property faces environmental challenges, including being located within a flood hazard zone, containing wetlands, and being near the Chesapeake Bay Resource Protection Area.
*   **City's Perspective:** The City of Virginia Beach owns the Parks Avenue property and has been exploring alternative uses for the land.

> "The building needs roughly \$900,000 in repairs and maintenance work, and the land has significant environmental constraints, according to the city."

The move to Virginia Wesleyan offers the MOCA a custom-built facility with expanded education and exhibition space, allowing it to better serve its mission and the community. The **Virginia MOCA Bids Farewell** to these challenges.

### What's Next for MOCA at Virginia Wesleyan?

The Virginia MOCA's new home at Virginia Wesleyan University promises exciting opportunities for growth and innovation. The custom-built facility will expand the museum's education and exhibition space by 20%, providing more room to showcase contemporary art and engage with the community. The **Virginia MOCA Bids Farewell** and looks ahead to new opportunities.

The museum is scheduled to open to the public at its new location on April 18th, and the inaugural exhibition will feature the work of contemporary painter Nina Chanel Abney.

## The Future of 2200 Parks Avenue

With the Virginia MOCA moving to Virginia Wesleyan University, the question arises: what's next for the 9.6-acre property at 2200 Parks Avenue? As the **Virginia MOCA Bids Farewell**, the community wonders what's next for the space.

### The City's Plans for the Former MOCA Site

The City of Virginia Beach owns the Parks Avenue property and has been exploring options for its future use. In April, the city issued a request for ideas (RFI) to lease the property, receiving nine proposals. However, in September, the City Council chose to cancel the solicitation and explore other options through an advisory group, according to a city spokesperson. The **Virginia MOCA Bids Farewell**, and the city reconsiders the space.

> "Last April, the city issued a request for ideas to lease it and received nine proposals. But in September, the City Council chose to cancel the solicitation and explore other options through an advisory group, according to a city spokesperson."

The cancellation of the RFI has left the future of the Parks Avenue property uncertain, but it also presents an opportunity for innovative ideas to emerge.
